Conflicts of Interest
As an employee, it’s very important that you avoid conflicts of interest that could influence your judgment or put your loyalty to the company at risk.

Conflicts of interest happen when someone’s decision-making on behalf of the company is influenced by their personal relationships or pursuits.

Of course, employees do and should maintain private lives outside of work. To stay clear of conflicts of interest, it’s essential that you avoid any situations that could put you in the position of having to choose between your personal interests and those of the company.

Avoiding Conflicts of Interest
To avoid creating a conflict of interest, or even creating the appearance of one, a few things are especially important.

Click the options to learn more about situations that might give the appearance of a conflict of interest.

Family
Don’t participate in any business decision involving a company for which your spouse or a family member works.

Job
Don’t take a second job that could affect your ability to perform your responsibilities on behalf of this company.

Gifts
And don’t accept any gift from a customer, supplier, or service provider that doesn’t meet the standards of our business gift guidelines.

Gifts, Gratuities, and Entertainment
Offering or accepting gifts, gratuities, or entertainment can create a conflict of interest, or the appearance of one, between an employee’s personal interests and their responsibilities to the company.

To make sure this doesn’t happen, employees should never accept gifts, gratuities, or entertainment from anyone they believe to be attempting to influence the company’s business decisions.

Click the options to see some examples of someone that might influence a business decision.

Suppliers or vendors

Potential suppliers or vendors

Government officials

Any other person who might be seeking influence

Business Entertainment
We understand that business entertainment is sometimes important in strengthening working relationships among our employees and those with whom they transact business on the company’s behalf – meals, sporting events, and other social outings can be the glue that holds a business relationship together.

Employees are permitted to offer or accept reasonable business entertainment that’s related to legitimate business purposes, in compliance with our policy. To make sure you know what’s appropriate and what’s not, consult our company’s policy for more information.

HOST: Intellectual property is among any company's most valuable assets. All employees are expected to utilize our intellectual property in a way that preserves and protects the company's interests. It is also our policy that the company and its employees will respect and avoid infringement upon the intellectual property rights of any third parties, such as customers, vendors, or competitors.

Intellectual property may include a wide variety of assets.

Copyrights protect original works of authorship, providing authors and copyright owners with exclusive rights to publish, produce, reproduce, and sell their work. Copyrights apply to many different types of original creative works.

Patents protect original or new inventions. A patent can prevent others from making, using, or selling an invention, or a product manufactured to an invention, without the inventor's or patent owner's permission.

Industrial designs are protected if they are new or original. In some jurisdictions, industrial designs are protected as design patents.

Trademarks are identifiers of goods, and, in some jurisdictions, also services, and their trade origin. They include words, phrases, slogans, logos, graphics, sounds, and colors.

Service marks, like trademarks, are identifiers. However, service marks relate specifically to services rather than products.

Trade secrets are information that a business keeps secret to give it a competitive advantage. Trade secrets can include formulas, ingredients, and processes.

In some jurisdictions trade secrets are part of a wider concept of confidential information.

In short, intellectual property is the lifeblood of the company, necessary for its continued growth and success. It must be vigilantly protected and secured.

1.  [Topic title: Personally Identifiable Information (PII).] Personally Identifiable Information, known as PII, is generally defined as information which can be used to distinguish or trace an individual's identity, either alone or in combination with other information which is linked to a specific individual.

Examples of PII that can directly identify an individual on their own include full name, physical address, government identification number, telephone number, or e-mail address. Information that is “linked to a specific individual” could include date and place of birth or a person's mother's maiden name.

Individuals can also be identified through indirect PII, which may not specifically identify an individual in and of itself, but when combined with other information, can become PII from which an individual can be clearly identified. Some examples of indirect PII include sex, race, geographic indicators, and other secondary descriptors. Additionally, public information that permits the physical or online contacting of a specific individual is another form of indirect PII.

Organizations that handle or manage PII have a legal duty to protect it from loss, theft, or improper use. Both individuals and organizations can experience serious harm from a PII breach. Individual damages may include identity theft, loss of privacy, and financial loss. Organizational damages may include legal liability, remediation costs, and loss of the public's trust.

Companies and their employees must take steps to guard against PII breaches. In addition to investing in technology and equipment, organizations implement policies and procedures that govern the way PII and other sensitive information is collected, stored, processed, and disposed of.
As an employee, you must ensure that you understand and follow these policies and procedures as you go about your work.

Avoiding Phishing Attacks
Learning Objective

1. Learning Point 1
   [Video description begins] Topic title: Avoiding Phishing Attacks. [Video description ends]
   In today's digitally connected world, it's critical for you to be aware of the methods scammers may use to steal your personal data and the data of the organization where you work.

"Phishing" is one of the oldest and most common scams hackers use to do this. Phishing scams involve sending fake e-mails to lure people into sharing sensitive data. At first glance, the messages may seem like legitimate requests for information, possibly even from trusted people or companies. The scammer's intent is to trick recipients into transmitting sensitive information such as login credentials, financial details, or confidential company information. These scams often involve enticing the receiver to click on a link or attachment in the e-mail, which will then self-install a virus or malware program that opens up an entry point into the company's network.

Every month, approximately 100,000 new phishing attacks are reported. With thousands of attacks being launched every day, some are bound to be successful. In fact, the year 2019 alone saw $57 million lost by people falling prey to phishing scams.

Phishing scams have a reported overall 1 in 10 chance of success, and those odds aren't bad for a scam that requires very little work on the hacker's part. It's no surprise then that as people and companies continue to increase their reliance on technology, phishing attacks are becoming more prevalent – and more sophisticated.

As an employee, you are susceptible to phishing attacks on your company. Sometimes phishing e-mails intentionally target a specific person or department, while other times they are randomly sent to multiple employees who often assume e-mails are safe if they are not flagged by the company's spam filters. And that's the whole point of a phishing attack – to make the fake message seem legitimate so that as many people as possible will fall prey to it.

Phishing attacks are often successful because the e-mail comes directly to you, is sent from what appears to be a sender you know or do business with, and often contains a subject line that piques your interest, such as "Re: our discussion yesterday." You may know you didn't have a discussion with the sender yesterday, but because it's coming from a contact you're familiar with, you may wonder what this message could be about and open it.

In the past, hackers used e-mails containing harmful links for their phishing attempts, and they were often very easy to spot. But phishing techniques have evolved and have become much harder to recognize. Each day, scammers continue to find new ways to deceive recipients.

There are multiple forms that phishing attacks can take, so it's important to be able to differentiate legitimate e-mail requests from those that may be malicious attempts to get information. Here are some things to keep in mind.

First, phishing e-mails may appear to be from recognized senders. Hackers might imitate people you know and trust in order to deceive you. By imitating a recognized sender, hackers increase the chances that users will fall for their phishing schemes.

Attacks may also imitate a legitimate provider. The most common type of phishing attack seems to come from companies you know and trust, like banks, online stores, or entertainment providers like Netflix. They will most likely attempt to get you to interact with them by conveying a sense of urgency, perhaps with a limited-time sale or a problem with your account.

Phishing attacks often make up some sort of story to get you to interact with harmful links or attachments. This kind of story-based scam is known as pretexting. These e-mails usually present you with a problem that can only be fixed by interacting with them, like asking you to confirm some personal information. They might even provide links to give you money or discounts.

Beware of e-mails containing personalized information. Many phishing attacks are personally customized to individuals and can include personal details like your name and job title to trick you into believing the message is important and trustworthy.

Finally, beware of hyperlinks. Many phishing e-mails will contain a link that, once clicked, can infect your computer with malware or redirect you to a separate website where the hacker will be able to gain access to your sensitive data.

If you have concerns about an e-mail, contact the sender by another means to confirm its validity. For example, don't interact with an e-mail that appears to be from your bank alerting you to a problem with your account; instead, call your bank directly to ask if there's a problem.

Proper Password Management
Learning Objective

identify best practices for creating and managing passwords

1. Learning Point 1
   [Video description begins] Topic title: Proper Password Management. [Video description ends]
   Passwords are used to protect a wide variety of electronic resources, from your personal bank account's online portal to your company's network infrastructure. Though they are the front line of data defense, poor password security practices and data breaches are all too common.

In 2018, Verizon's Data Breach Investigations Report found that 81% of hacking-related data breaches, were due to stolen or weak passwords. This alarming statistic points to the dangers posed by unsafe password practices.

According to a 2019 report compiled from a survey of 1,761 IT and IT security practitioners about end-user habits, other risks associated with poor password creation and management include sharing and reusing passwords. The report also noted that 69% of end users share passwords with colleagues to access accounts, and over half – 51% – reuse passwords across both their business and personal accounts.

Additionally, 57% of individuals who had experienced phishing attacks did not change their password behaviors in spite of the possibility that their credentials may have been stolen. And even though two-factor authentication increases password security, 67% don't use it for their personal logins, and 55% don't use it at work. And not surprisingly, 57% expressed a preference for a login method that does not involve the use of passwords at all.

Cybercriminals employ various techniques to crack passwords. For example, some hackers use software that automates the process of repetitively attempting common words in password fields, in what are known as "dictionary attacks."

Another technique is cracking easy security questions. Many people use a spouse's, a child's, or pet's name for their security questions and answers. The first problem with this approach is obvious: hackers know people commonly use this type of information. And they also know people use this type of information not only for answers to the security questions that many online accounts require, but also for passwords themselves. The second problem is that hackers know much of this information can be found on social media profiles or deduced with a little research.

Hackers are also good at guessing simple passwords. What do you think is the most popular password according to BeyondTrust.com? If you said 123456, you're right. And the next most popular password is 12345.

Another poor password security practice is the reuse of passwords across multiple sites. Single password usage across personal e-mail, banking, and social media accounts increases the chance that if a hacker cracks your password, you'll become a victim of identity theft.

Social engineering – a technique used by scammers to deceive and manipulate people into divulging confidential or personal information – is another tactic hackers use to trick people into disclosing username and password details.

The technology industry continually updates recommended processes and best practices using their research into how hackers break into accounts to help individuals and organizations defend against these attacks. Here are the seven recommended best practices for creating and managing passwords.

First, and most important, follow your company's password management policies. You may choose to apply additional password security steps if you like, but at minimum, the requirements in your company's password management policy must be followed.

Use unique passwords for each account, service, and system. To minimize the damage a hacker can do to your accounts if a password is stolen, use a different password for each account you have, as well as the services and systems you use. Do not duplicate, reuse, or copy your passwords.

Use strong, longer passphrases; do not use common words or easily guessed passwords. Generally accepted best practices for passwords include using at least 8 but no more than 64 characters, though this may vary depending on your organization's particular policies. Include special characters in addition to letters and numbers, and don't repeat any character in succession.

Avoid using common or singular "dictionary" words, and don't use names of people in your personal life or any personally identifiable numeric data such as your birthday, government identification number, or driver's license number.

If your employer permits it, and one is available, use a password manager app. Password manager services let you store passwords electronically. This means you can create strong, unique passwords for as many accounts as you have without all the frustration and delays that come with forgotten passwords.

Another best practice is to make sure you keep all your passwords private at all times. Don't share your passwords with anybody, either in full or partially. Don't leave your username and password credentials in a medium that is unsafe – for example, don't e-mail your credentials to yourself, or write your password on a piece of paper stored in your desk drawer. Also, remember to log off of public computers and remove your account from the computer's history. If your credentials are compromised in any way, or you feel there is even the possibility they have been compromised, change your passwords immediately.

And finally, report all suspected or confirmed password breaches or attempts to obtain this information to your IT helpdesk immediately.

Insider Threats
Learning Objective

1. Learning Point 1
   Insider threats arise when employees with otherwise legitimate access to an organization's networks, systems, and data cause harm through either mistakes or the deliberate misuse of that access. In this topic, we'll focus on the latter – deliberate, malicious misuse of access. Employees may have any number of motivations for exploiting their access to company networks to cause harm. Personal gain is one such motivation. An employee could, for example, cooperate with an unscrupulous competitor of the company to steal intellectual property in exchange for a financial payment. Or an employee who feels they have been wronged by the company may release sensitive information in an attempt to "get back at" the employer. Insider threats are a serious concern for organizations because they are difficult to predict and detect. The number of insider threats to companies is rising and they are an increasingly common cause of data breaches. A key defense against insider threats is the commitment by employees to follow the company's IT and data access policies. These policies may include two specific controls designed to reduce the likelihood of an insider incident – least privilege and separation of duties. Least privilege restricts access and use of systems, applications, and data to only those employees who require access as part of their job duties, and only to the minimum level required for those duties to be carried out. Separation of duties ensures high-risk tasks are set up to require the involvement of two or more people in order for the task to be completed. Each employee assigned to the task is provided with only the access needed to complete their individual part of it. The idea is to reduce the damage that a single malicious employee can achieve.

recognize key best physical and digital security practices to follow when working in public places

1. Learning Point 1
   Digital technology offers the freedom of working remotely from anywhere and even when on the go. This enables employees to conduct work in public places like cafes, restaurants, airports, buses, and trains. Though convenient, this flexibility comes with data security challenges employees should be aware of when working in public places. Here are a few key practices to keep in mind. First, before you begin remote work, confirm with your company that you are allowed to do so, specifically in public places. If you have approval, it's imperative that you comply with all of your employer's remote work policies. Review the policies and consult with the IT helpdesk if you have questions. When working remotely, there are two types of security to consider: digital and physical. Digital security best practices for working in public places include using only secured Wi-Fi network connections. In addition, provided that your employer permits it, it's a good practice to use a VPN, which encrypts the data between your device and the company's network, protecting it from being intercepted by cyber criminals. Best practices for physical security when working in public places include never leaving your devices unattended, even momentarily. This helps protect against the risk of loss or theft of the devices and the data they hold. Always keep your devices on your person and locked when not in active use. Be aware of your physical surroundings and the proximity of others. Ensure your device screens are not visible to anyone nearby. Consider using a privacy screen to reduce its visibility to others. The same applies to phone calls or video chats made in public places – consider using headphones or ear buds to ensure that no one around you can hear what the meeting participants are saying during the meeting.

Ensuring Physical Security
Learning Objective
After completing this topic, you should be able to

identify physical data security best practices

1. Learning Point 1
   Your role in data security involves more than just safe online practices. The physical security of the devices that contain data, as well as the buildings and facilities in which those devices are located, is an equally important priority for you to be aware of. A substantial amount of data is lost to hackers and other bad actors because of failures in physical security practices. There are a number of key physical data security best practices you can follow to help keep your company's data, network, systems, and infrastructure safe. Perimeter security measures can limit physical access to your worksite or premises, which in turn will help to prevent physical access by unauthorized individuals. The risk is that anyone who gains access to the premises could engage in criminal behavior such as stealing a laptop or other device containing sensitive information, physically sabotaging equipment, or connecting a device containing malware to the network through an IP port. To guard against this, ensure that you follow your employer's security protocols when entering the building. Resist the urge to hold the door open for someone behind you. While polite, doing so could defeat the building's perimeter security measures. Also, never prop open exterior doors or do anything to disable the security features of any door or window. Follow your company's procedure for registering visitors to the premises. Even non-employees you know well and who visit regularly should register every time. Once inside the building, use caution around any restricted areas where you may have access. Be especially aware of "tailgating," which happens when an unauthorized person enters a restricted area by closely following someone with access. If you observe any unescorted visitors or notice any suspicious behavior around restricted areas, contact security immediately. It's also a good practice to properly secure all devices containing company data, like PCs, laptops, USB drives and other removable media storage devices. Be sure to follow your employer's procedures for locking or otherwise securing each type of device. Always maintain a close watch on portable electronic devices like tablets and smartphones. Do not leave these devices unattended or unsecured on your desk, in conference rooms, break rooms, or other common areas. And when you take these mobile devices with you out of the office, use the same level of care. Don't, for example, leave devices in unlocked cars or any place in public where they could be stolen. Finally, remember that paper records are also at risk for data loss. Safeguard all documents containing sensitive information by using, transporting, and storing them securely. Be careful when using photocopiers not to leave originals or copies behind. And be sure to dispose of documents properly when no longer needed by shredding or otherwise destroying them according to your employer's policy.

Preventing Data Loss and Theft
Learning Objective
After completing this topic, you should be able to

recognize techniques for preventing data loss or theft

1. Learning Point 1
   Information is among an organization's most valuable assets, and a company that experiences data loss or theft faces significant negative results. The average cost of a data breach in 2020 was $3.86 million. And the harm to a company's reputation can be irreparable. Companies go to great lengths to protect the various types of data and information they collect and create. This information can cover a wide range of types, from design specifications, formulas, and product details, to financial reports, customer lists, and market research, to personal information about employees and customers. All of it must be protected. Most companies invest considerable resources into data loss prevention tools and processes to help prevent loss and theft. However, employees play just as critical a role in protecting the security of a company's data. There are several best practices you can follow to help prevent data breaches and protect your company's information. First, read and comply with the company's IT policies. Be sure you understand how these policies and any related data security procedures apply to your job role and the information that you routinely use. If you are unsure how a policy applies to you, ask your IT department. Next, follow your company's data backup procedures. You may, for example, be required to store files on a shared server to enable nightly backups by IT. Or, if local file storage is permitted, you may be required to use a utility for periodic backup. If you have any questions about data backup, contact your IT department's helpdesk for guidance. Also, comply with all corporate rules for data encryption when sending or storing information. Another best practice is to comply with all company device-use policies. For example, your company may prohibit transmitting certain sensitive data over mobile devices, or there may be restrictions on using your own smartphone or tablet for work. Check the policy to see what restrictions might apply. And ensure every device is protected by a password that meets your company's stringency requirements. Finally, be aware of the physical security of your workplace and devices. Follow all protocols related to door access, visitor registration, and restricted areas. Safeguard your electronic devices, and be mindful of storage practices for documents containing sensitive information. Use caution when working in public places or when traveling with network-connected devices. By following these best practices, you can help do you part to protect the company's valuable information.
